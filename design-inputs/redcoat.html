What if there were apps for modern women's groups?

"Though women . . . don't have to engage in that sort of competion [that men have in men's groups], they also don't have the opportunity to change their position in the group's heirarchy."

Deborah Tannen in You're the Only One I Can Tell

Despite any view of women's groups, women find membership in a supportive group to be one of the the greatest sources of satisfaction in their lives.

How can we leverage technology to give more and more women a positive group experience? Who better to answer that question than a group of technical women?

How the Data Scientista Society Dreamed a Dream

You may have navigated to this page via a hyperlink from anywhere. Notice, you haven't landed on the pitch page of some snazzy startup. Instead, you have found a website of a group of women dedicated to supporting one another against all odds. They have found, however, that their growth is delayed without automating their processes. They could continue to hobble along, or, they could apply their talents for enabling women's group everywhere. Here are three functions of a desired app.

Attendance Survey-to-Newsletter Pipeline

It has been at each meeting, that members sign in, along with completing a questionnaire, with such questions as, Are you looking for a job? and Are you hiring for a job? The organizer would collect these hand-signed surveys at the end of every meeting and manually compile them into a monthly newsletter, which included news items about the group as well. This monthly newsletter not only created a sense of community, but helped women find jobs, proving that tech women can and do support one another. Manually creating the newsletter is burdensome. However, creating the app to take attendance and collect survey data, and then convert all data into simple newsletter is doable.

Attendance Analytics to Accelerate Consensus

What can be gleaned from attendance data? Assuming attendance is a subsitute variable for eye contact, we can use techniques from social network analysis to rank or weight members. This weighting can be used to prioritize, recognize, and reward the most active members — which is exactly what eye contact does in the first place. The ranking can be used for many purposes: identify potential leaders based on the commitment of their attendance; create a reimbursement queue; distribute limited perks; quantify consensus. In the end, the ranking makes leadership less burdensome, so leadership is less likely to burn out. Members can remain engaged, because their place in the heirarchy is not fixed, but changeable, based on thier attendance. All of this is possible through an app that easily collects attendance data.

Attribute Data to Grow and Leverage Competencies

All support groups and success groups exist to enable the members to arise from some state A to a better state B. Usually, better B members impart their wisdom and knowledge. In the case of career groups, skills and other attributes are already captured in LinkedIn data. With an app to which members may upload their LinkedIn data, the data can then be compiled and analyzed. Clustering techniques will allow to identify a group of B members with an expertise to share with A members. The group can also analyze the data to make sure programs are successfully converting A members to B members, for any given attribute.



Next Steps

We hope you are familiar with the mantra one day at a time, as this is a group of volunteers. However, we will be reaching out to find partners. Meanwhile, here are the next steps.

Survey about Women's Groups

We have received advice to use Lean Startup principles. One interpretation is to create a survey for women about women's groups. This survey would not suggest that there is a product or solution to women's group woes. The survey would simply explore women's involvement and outcomes with women's groups, such as longevity and satisfaction, and use of technology. We would use the survey to prove the existence of a market of technology to meet the needs of women's groups.

Future Fair Alliance

It is important to cultivate a culture of entire tech women's groups working together. Many different skills, technical or otherwise, will be necessary for a successful project. Towards this end, an event called the Future Fair begins to introduce groups to one another and create the first communication channels, laying the pathway for an invitation to join and opensource project.

Investment Proposition

Conceivably, the development of the minimum viable product and its iterations could appear much faster with funds to pay developers. We are beginning to learn about attracting funding. So, let us describe the project's aspects that an investor would want to understand.

Target Customer

While women's groups is the core market of this application, the true target market is any success group, support group, or community empowerment group that is likewise volunteer dependent, cooporative, connecting, and exists to get members from an observable state A to state B. A few examples are activist groups, benefit societies, guilds, neighborhood associations, support groups, support groups for caretakers.

Market Plan

The plan is that, once we have a minimum viable product (MVP) for the attendand-to-newsletter pipeline, we invite the alliance of women's groups who participated in the Future Fair to beging using the app; other tech women would challenge the app the most.

Revenue Plan

Once the MVP is stable, and has a value proposition for non-technical women's groups, we begin to invite other local women's groups to use the app, at a small fee per person, per group. Example networks we may pitch to could be the local Girl Scout network or the Women's League. Insights made possible through the apps data could help the respective groups grow.

The data belongs to the group that supplies it. However, the possiblity remains for any group to donate its data to be used for further purposes. For example, the Data Scientista Soceity may license its data to be used in school that teaches women data science. A legacy opportunity is clearly created: while there has been much analysis of largely male sports teams and armed forces, there is little study of women's groups. Humanity has a missing pocket of knowledge that can be filled. In support of this project, the Data Scientista Society is fulfilling its vision of a world full of data of the women, by the women, for the women.

    • Competition

    There are platforms that are for groups, or social platforms that offer a side dish of group, but all these platforms fall short of supporting the leaders who hold themsleves responsible for growing the group. When the leader quits from exhaustion, or the group quits an unsupported leader, what happens next?

    Meetup takes attendance, but does not complete the cycle with a newletter/communication piece. Alerts and bulletin boards are far from the structured, routine communication that builds a sense of community and grows the group. Facebook is ubiqutious, but doesn't allow data-driven management of the group. Git fosters contributing community, but that community is for technical people doing technical work, not women voting on books for book club, or groups making decisions nearly as formally as Robert's Rules. LinkedIn, with good conscience, doesn't provide a (non-recruiter) group leader access to combined profile data in analytics. All of these platforms can serve a group, but what is necessary is a central app glueing them together to ensure group growth.
